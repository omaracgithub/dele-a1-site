DELE A1 Listening Comprehension — How to Prepare Your Ear from Scratch

The DELE A1 listening comprehension test lasts only 20 minutes, has 4 tasks, and 25 items in total. The audios are very slow, with clear pronunciation, and cover basic topics: phone numbers, addresses, greetings, and introductions.

If you have never listened to Spanish in a formal exam, don't worry. The A1 audios are designed for people who have been studying for only a few weeks or months. Each audio is played twice.

Test Structure

Task 1: Messages and Announcements

You will hear very short messages: an announcement at a train station, a message on an answering machine, or a notice in a store. Each one lasts between 15 and 30 seconds.

What you need to catch:

  • Numbers: schedules, prices, phone numbers
  • Places: "at door 3", "second floor"
  • Simple actions: "the train leaves at...", "the store closes at..."

Strategy: Before the audio plays, read the answer options. Identify what type of information you need (a number, a place, a time). This way, your brain knows what to look for.

Task 2: Brief Dialogues

Two people talk about something specific: ordering in a restaurant, asking for directions, making a purchase. The dialogues have 3-5 exchanges and use very basic vocabulary.

Strategy: Pay attention to the second person in the dialogue. Usually, the exam question refers to what one of the speakers responds. If they ask, "What does the man want?", the answer is often in his last sentence.

Task 3: Informative Monologues

One person talks about themselves or describes something: their family, their house, their job, their daily routine. They speak slowly and with clear pauses.

Typical information you should identify:

  • Name and age
  • Where they live
  • What they like
  • Family members
  • Their job or studies

Strategy: Write down numbers and proper names while you listen. These are objective data that you can catch even if you don't understand the entire context.

Task 4: Everyday Conversations

A slightly longer dialogue about everyday topics: plans for the weekend, a purchase in a store, organizing a meal. The vocabulary remains A1 but the sentences are somewhat longer.

Strategy: Don't try to understand every word. Look for the specific information that the question asks for. If they ask, "When are they going to the movies?", you only need to catch the day or time.

What Makes This Test Difficult for Beginners

Although the audios are slow and clear, there are three typical problems:

  • The numbers: Confusing "fifteen" (15) with "fifty" (50), or "sixty" with "seventy".
  • The reading speed: Not having time to read the options before the audio plays.
  • The panic: Losing one answer and getting stuck instead of moving on to the next.

How to Train Your Ear from Scratch

Weeks 1-2: Sounds and Numbers

  • Listen to the Spanish alphabet aloud every day
  • Practice numbers from 1 to 100 with audios
  • Repeat aloud everything you hear

Weeks 3-4: Short Phrases

  • Listen to greetings and introductions ("Hello, my name is..., I am from..., I am... years old")
  • Play podcasts for absolute beginners (very slow speed)
  • Try to write down what you hear (dictation)

Weeks 5-8: Exam Format

  • Do listening comprehension tasks with official models
  • Practice with a timer (20 minutes for the 4 tasks)
  • Listen to each audio only twice, as in the real exam

Tips for Exam Day

  • Use the seconds of pause between audios to read the next questions.
  • On the first listen, try to catch the general idea. On the second, look for the specific detail.
  • If you miss an answer, mark it randomly and move on. Going back mentally makes you lose more answers.
  • Numbers are the easiest data to catch. Make sure you master the numbers from 1 to 1000.
